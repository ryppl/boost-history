Description and Implementation

Modified Bessel functions of fractional order (i.e. order νis any real number) are much harder to compute than modified Bessel functions of integer order. Though recurrence relations are the same, the difficulty lies in the initial values.

The function domains are  Iν(x): (0, ∞),  Kν(x): (0, ∞).

To get the initial values, we first normalize ν to [0, ∞) with the help of reflection formulas. Let μ = ν - [ν + 1/2] where [x] is the floor function, then μ is the fractional part of ν such that |μ| <= 1/2 (we need this for convergence later). The idea is to calculate  Kμ(x),  Kμ+1(x) and use them to obtain  Iν(x),  Kν(x).

The algorithm is proposed by Temme [18], which needs two continued fractions as well as the Wronskian.

The continued fractions are computed using the modified Lentz's method [13]. Their convergence rates depend on x, therefore we need different strategies for large x and small x.

x > v, CF1 needs O(x) iterations to converge, CF2 converges rapidly

x <= v, CF1 converges rapidly, CF2 fails to converge when x -> 0

Note

Analogous to bessel_jy(v, x, &J, &Y), bessel_ik(v, x, &I, &K) computes  Iν and  Kν simultaneously.

Testing

The test driver calculates relative errors and the following table lists some typical values.

Function float double long double
bessel_iv(x) ~ 10-8 , 10-7  ~ 10-16 , 10-15  ~ 10-18 , 10-17 
bessel_kv(x) ~ 10-8 , 10-7  ~ 10-16 , 10-15  ~ 10-18 , 10-17 

The accuracy may be sensitive to data type precision. For example, the relative errors of  I-10.0003(0.01) and  K-5.5(100) are about 10-3 and 10-1 when float is used. But they reduce to 10-12 and 10-16 for double, and 10-16 and 10-17 for long double.
